Fundamentals

The Nature of Sound

The sense of sound is the next most prominent awareness we have. Understanding its fundamental properties is the first step to designing with it.

Temporal

Sound takes time — it's temporal by nature. It unfolds, builds, and fades.

Multi-dimensional

Sound occupies space in pitch, volume, timbre, and direction simultaneously.

Cyclical

Sound is rhythmic by nature — patterns of repetition and variation create meaning.

Emotional

Unlike visuals, sound has a direct and immediate connection to our feelings.

Sound Perception vs. Vision

We can detect changes in sound at a rate of 44,000 times per second. The finest resolution our eyes can perceive is about 50 frames per second. Your ears are nearly 1,000× faster than your eyes.

A child in the dark, gripped with fear, comforts himself by singing under his breath. He walks and halts to his song. Lost, he takes shelter, or orients himself with his little song as best he can. The song is like a rough sketch of a calming and stabilizing, calm and stable, center in the heart of chaos.

Core Concept

Sound ≠ Reality

Sound in film doesn't need to be accurate. It doesn't need to be a 1:1 copy of the real world. So what is sound for?

Sound = How a Character Experiences the World

The physical world stays the same. What changes is the character's relationship with it. Sound design captures that relationship — not the world itself, but how someone feels the world in a given moment.

Same person, same city, same sounds around them. But their emotional state changes everything they hear. Imagine one day, four emotions — each reshaping the entire soundscape around a single character.

Exercise in Perception

One Day, Four Emotions

Four scenes. One world. Four soundscapes. The physical world didn't change — the sound design did.

Scene 01

Tension — An Exam

The room is full of sounds — air conditioning, pages flipping, chairs creaking. But you're stuck on a question. Anxiety kicks in. Suddenly the clock is deafening. Someone turning a page sounds sharp and irritating. The sounds haven't changed. Your state has.

Scene 02

Focus — Completely Absorbed

You're drawing, writing, designing. You enter the zone. Every sound around you fades, becomes distant and muffled. Your world shrinks to just the sound of your hand moving. Everything else is behind a wall of glass.

Scene 03

Joy — Walking Outside

You push the door open. The world rushes in — wind, voices, rustling leaves, a tram passing. Everything sounds clear, rich, alive. You've opened up again. You're receiving the world.

Scene 04

Intimacy — Being Quietly With Someone

A café on a rainy day. The ambient sounds are soft and warm. Rain becomes comfortable white noise. The other person's voice is close and clear. Everything else naturally falls into the background.

The Takeaway

Sound design doesn't record the world. It records how a person experiences the world in that moment. Four scenes, one world — but four completely different soundscapes, each shaped by the character's inner state.

Sound Design Toolkit

Categories of Sound Design

The core building blocks of cinematic sound design. Each serves a specific narrative and emotional function.

Drones & Atmospheres

Set the sentiment or motion — uplifting or intense — for a scene.

Risers

Build over time, creating suspense and anticipation with a steep drop.

Slow Motion

Designed to convey the slowing down of time and heightened awareness.

Time-lapse

Convey the rush and speeding up of time passing quickly.

Transitions

Classic pushes, sat-backs, and hits that bridge between scenes.

Ambient

Environmental textures like wind, forests, rain, and room tone.

Build Up

Layered intensity. High dynamic range from silence to full power.

High Dynamic Range

Dramatic contrast between the quietest and loudest moments.

Editing Techniques

Sound Bridges

J-Cuts and L-Cuts are essential dialogue sound bridge techniques that create seamless transitions between scenes.

Dialogue Sound Bridge

L-Cut

Audio from the current scene continues playing over the visual of the next scene. The viewer sees the new image but still hears the previous speaker — creating emotional carry-over.

Dialogue Sound Bridge

J-Cut

Audio from the next scene begins before its visual appears. The viewer hears the upcoming scene while still seeing the current one — building anticipation and pulling them forward.

Expressionism in Sound

Just as harsh shadows and distorted set design can externalize a character's psychological state in visual expressionism, sound can express these internal states as well. Distortion, echo, silence, and amplification all become tools for making the invisible inner world audible.

Sound Exercise

Listening to Your Space

You've already filmed your space. Now go back — but this time, listen. Record the sonic identity of your space, build a sound palette of its materials and textures, then layer sound onto your existing footage in Premiere Pro. The challenge: making the same visuals feel completely different through sound alone.

Why this matters: Sound doesn't copy reality — it captures how a person experiences the world in a given moment. The same corridor can feel peaceful or unsettling, intimate or vast, depending on what you hear. As spatial designers, you already sense the acoustic character of a room when you walk into it. This week you make that sensing audible.

01

Go Back to Your Space and Listen

Visit your chosen space. Don't film. Sit quietly for five minutes and just listen — room tone, material sounds, distant activity, silence. Notice what you've never noticed before.

15 min
02

Record a Spatial Sound Portrait

Using your phone, record 1 minute of audio that best captures the "character" of this space. This is audio only — no video needed. Think of it as a sonic identity card for your space.

15 min
03

Build a Sound Palette

Think of this as a material board, but for sound. Record 5–8 individual sound clips from your space, each 10–15 seconds. For example:

A door closing, footsteps on the floor
A tap running, a window opening
The hum of an appliance, the creak of a chair
Sounds leaking in from outside

Organise them into a folder with clear file names (e.g. door-close.m4a, footsteps-tile.m4a). These become your raw sonic materials for the weeks ahead.

Tips for Your Sound Layers

Approaches for making sound serve your spatial narrative.

Room Tone as Foundation
Record the "silence" of your space — every room has its own baseline hum. Lay this underneath everything as a bed. It grounds the viewer in the space even when other sounds change.
Sound Reveals What Camera Can't
A dripping tap, a distant train, footsteps upstairs — these tell the viewer about scale, material, and life beyond the frame.
J-Cut for Spatial Transitions
Let the sound of the next space arrive before the image cuts. The viewer starts "entering" the new space through their ears before their eyes follow.
Emotion Through Volume
The relative volume between sounds matters more than the absolute volume of any single sound. Pulling ambient sound down can create focus; bringing it back up can create release.
