The Problem

Same film,
two different sentences.

This is what most students do the first time they write a prompt — type out the feeling in their head, hit Enter, get a "decent" image, drop it into the Pitch Deck, and move on.

But here's the problem: the poster you imagined? AI never saw it.

Prompt A · By Feel
a mysterious library poster, cinematic, atmospheric
Prompt B · Structured
an old library that rises and falls with the tides, half-submerged each night, an elderly librarian floating beneath the water surface, deep ocean blue 80% + moonlight silver + book-spine gold...

Same AI, same tool. The entire difference is in those words.

Core Insight

AI doesn't understand.
It only matches.

The phrase on the left, AI has seen millions of times — so it gives you an average library, the kind everyone has seen before. The phrase on the right is a combination AI hasn't encountered. It has to recombine multiple concepts from scratch — that's how the image becomes distinctive.

Vague words → mediocre images.
Specific words → distinctive images.

Common Mistakes

Words AI can't translate.

The phrases below have images in your head — but AI doesn't know what those images look like. They're feelings, not pictures.

a cool character
cinematic light
a mysterious color palette

The rest of this guide is one thing: how to translate feelings into pictures AI can understand. The method is just one — the Five-Layer Prompt.

Layer 01

Subject Anchor —
one sentence about your film.

Formula

[Time/Place] + [Character] + [Core conflict or event]

Hong Kong, 1962. Two neighbors, each betrayed by their spouse, develop a restrained connection in the narrow corridor of their old apartment building.

This sentence is the foundation. If you can't write it, your film isn't clear in your own mind yet. If you can write it, AI can help you. This layer isn't really for AI — it's for you. It forces you to answer one question: what am I actually making?

Layer 02

Composition Type —
pick a poster archetype.

Film posters aren't designed in a vacuum. They have classic skeletons. You don't have to choose only from these five, but you must choose one as your skeleton. Without one, the image becomes a mess.

Character Close-Up
Face or profile is the absolute subject
Atmospheric Space
Character placed in an emotional space
Minimal Symbol
One core object + large negative space
Double Exposure
Silhouette nesting a world inside
Collage Narrative
Multiple elements combined
Layer 03

Visual Elements —
what's actually in the frame.

This layer tests how well you understand your own film. Each element needs 2-3 distinctive adjectives. Every adjective with real information makes the image more unique.

a book
an open, self-illuminating, ancient leather-bound book with glowing pages
an old man
an elderly librarian with grey hair drifting slowly, wearing a deep-indigo woolen robe

Three element categories must all be covered: People (who, what posture, what clothing), Space (where, what materials, what state), and Signature Object (the key item that defines your film's worldview).

Layer 04 — Color

Color —
always specify ratios.

Without ratios, AI doesn't know which color leads. Colors get distributed evenly and the image goes muddy. Adding percentages is the simplest brute-force fix.

✓ Good · with ratios
Deep Ocean Blue · 80%
Moonlight Silver · 10%
Book-Spine Gold · 5%
Candlelight · 5%
✕ Bad · no ratios

"deep blue + silver + gold + candle yellow" → AI gives them equal weight → no dominant color → visual noise

Layer 04 — Light

Light —
the only source
of cinematic feel.

No light source = AI gives you flat lighting = poster looks like an ID photo. Light is the biggest single lever separating your poster from generic AI output. What the light passes through defines its texture.

i
Where it comes from
top-left / back-right /
overhead / backlit
ii
What color it is
warm yellow / cold white /
moonlight silver / neon pink
iii
What it passes through
smoke / glass /
water / blinds
cinematic light
a single warm-yellow source from the upper right, slanting down through smoke to form a visible shaft of light
Key Technique

Emotion —
translate it into visual fact.

AI doesn't know what "lonely" looks like — but it knows what "an isolated object + large negative space" looks like. Translate every emotion word into a specific visual fact.

create a feeling of loneliness
a phone booth standing alone in the center, 70% of the frame above is empty
a cozy home
a warm yellow lamp glowing, half a cup of tea still steaming, faded family photos hanging on the wall
an oppressive atmosphere
low ceiling, single bare bulb, water stains on the wall, furniture pushed tight against the walls

Counter-intuitive

Minimal style
actually needs more words.

This is what most students don't expect: if you don't write "avoid XX," AI fills the canvas by default — it thinks empty space is wrong. To make a minimal poster, you need more words telling AI what not to add.

Add this line

Avoid: excessive ornament, multiple elements, dramatic lighting, oversaturated color, complex backgrounds.

Maximalist
~ 400-word prompt
Minimal
~ 450-word prompt (incl. "avoid" clause)
Advanced Tricks

Five tricks that
separate great prompts
from average ones.

Once you've mastered the Five Layers, these are the moves that take your poster from "decent AI image" to "genuinely cinematic." Each one comes from studying prompts that work — not theory.

i
Era + Region + Medium = a style shortcut
Instead of describing every detail of a look, anchor it to a specific cultural moment. AI has seen massive volumes of imagery from these references and will load the entire aesthetic package at once — typography, paper texture, color logic, political mood, all of it.
Try1960s French New Wave theatrical poster · 1980s Hong Kong wuxia paperback cover · Edo-period ukiyo-e print · Bauhaus exhibition poster · 1990s Japanese cyberpunk anime cover
ii
Imperfection is texture
AI's default rendering is too clean. Real cinematic posters carry the marks of physical media — printing presses, film cameras, scanners, time. Actively request these "flaws" and your poster stops looking like AI output.
Tryslightly off-register ink · 35mm film grain · ink bleed at the edges · folded paper crease · dust and scratches · scan moiré · faded offset printing
iii
Two color rules — pick the right one
There are two ways to specify color. Choose the one that fits your style:
Ratio ruledeep ocean blue 80% + moonlight silver 10% + book-spine gold 5% + candle 5%
Logic rulehigh-contrast black-and-white imagery with selective red, blue, and yellow accents
Ratio rule fits atmospheric / cinematic posters. Logic rule fits pop, collage, minimalist, or graphic-design styles.
iv
Use directional adjectives, not neutral ones
Every adjective should imply what it's not. Neutral words ("a balanced composition") don't push AI in any particular direction. Directional words tell AI which way to lean.
Weakbalanced composition · cinematic typography · interesting design
Strongexpressive asymmetry · typography-led design · adventurous · graphic spontaneity · street-poster energy
v
Defensive prompting — block AI's bad habits
GPT Image 2 has known failure modes. Adding a short defensive line at the end prevents them before they happen.
TryKeep all visible text in English · No watermarks, no logos, no signatures · No human figures unless specified · No extra decorative characters or symbols

Self-Check

Don't generate yet —
self-check first.

After writing your prompt, run through the six questions below. If you can answer all of them, your prompt is solid. If you can't, go back and fill in the gaps. Never feed it straight to AI.

i
Can my L1 sentence make a stranger immediately understand my film? [Time/Place] + [Character] + [Core conflict] all included?
ii
Does my core object have 2-3 distinctive adjectives? "a book" → "open, self-illuminating, ancient leather-bound book"
iii
Are my colors given as ratios? main color 80% + secondary + accent — with percentages
iv
Does my light source say "where + color + passes through"? no light source = flat light = ID photo
v
Have I specified text position (top / mid / bottom + left / center / right)? each text line: what to write + where + which font
vi
If minimalist, did I write the "avoid" clause? without it, AI fills the canvas by default
