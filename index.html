Course Overview

Visual Layers

Layered Frames explores the art of video production, editing, and visual effects using Adobe Premiere Pro and After Effects. Students develop essential filmmaking techniques — from cinematic storytelling, lighting, and sound design to motion tracking, AI-assisted visuals, and advanced compositing.

Through practical projects, you will learn to construct visual narratives that articulate unique perspectives and creative visions. The course emphasizes the intersection of technical proficiency and artistic expression, exploring how digital media serves as a tool for storytelling and world-building in contemporary filmmaking.

Melbourne & Film

The Lost City of Melbourne

A city deeply intertwined with the origins of cinema.

In the 1850s, Melbourne was the fastest growing city in the world. It became an epicentre of film culture, and its hotels, restaurants, and cafes gained world renown.


However, the attempted "modernisation" of Melbourne in the 1950s destroyed much of the city, including its elegant cinemas and picture palaces. Buildings were deemed too Victorian — the opposite of a modern metropolis — and a demolition blitz began.


Featuring rare archival film and photography, the documentary The Lost City of Melbourne is a revelatory work that allows audiences to reimagine the former glory of the city.

The Story of the Kelly Gang

1906 · Melbourne

In 1906, Melbourne produced The Story of the Kelly Gang, recognized as the world's first feature-length narrative film. The city also hosts the Melbourne International Film Festival, launched in 1952 — one of the world's oldest — cementing its status as a global film culture hub.

Camera Movements

Essential camera movements that shape spatial perception and narrative tension in your films.

Static

Fixed frame. Stillness creates contemplation and draws focus to subject action.

REC

Tilt

Vertical pivot. Reveals height, scale, and power dynamics between subjects.

REC

Push In

Forward movement into the scene. Builds intensity, focus, and emotional engagement.

REC

Push Out

Backward movement from the scene. Reveals context, isolation, or conclusion.

Zoom

Optical focal length change. Compresses or expands perceived depth of space.

Dolly Zoom

Simultaneous zoom and dolly. Creates the disorienting "vertigo effect."

REC

Roll

Rotation on the lens axis. Conveys disorientation, dreaming, or shifting reality.

REC

Arc Shot

Circular movement around the subject. Creates dynamic spatial awareness.

REC

Tracking

Lateral movement alongside or behind subjects. Creates immersive spatial flow.

REC

Random

Handheld, unpredictable movement. Evokes chaos, urgency, or raw documentary feel.

Advanced Technique

Special Perspective Shooting

Placing the camera in unconventional positions to create immersive, voyeuristic, or narrative-driven viewpoints.

Embedded Camera

Camera placed in unconventional spots — inside a bag, refrigerator, or object — creating unique, restricted viewpoints that feel both intimate and voyeuristic.

Third-Person Perspective

A specialized form using embedded, object-based external view. Fixed frame constraints create a restrictive yet story-driven narrative perspective.

Action Camera Play

Using action cameras in creative mounting configurations — body-mounted, vehicle-attached, or suspended — for dynamic and immersive first-person footage.

Editing Exercise

Two Spaces, One Edit

In class you filmed inside the university building. Now go home and film in a completely different space. Then use Premiere Pro to combine both into a single cohesive edit. The challenge: making two unrelated locations feel like one story through editing alone.

Why this matters: Spatial narrative is constructed in the edit, not in the shoot. Two shots filmed in completely different buildings can feel continuous if the rhythm, movement, and visual logic connect them. This exercise trains that instinct from day one.

01

Review Your In-Class Footage

Watch what you filmed in class. Pick the strongest 3–5 clips — think about which camera movements and spatial moments work best.

20 min
02

Film at Home (or Any Space Outside Uni)

Shoot 5–8 new clips that extend or echo your in-class prompt. Use at least 3 different camera movements from what we covered (static, pan, tilt, push in/out, tracking, handheld, etc.).

Think spatially: How does this new space connect to the university space? It could be a continuation of the story, a contrast, a mirror, or a before/after.

1 hr
03

Edit in Premiere Pro

Combine your in-class footage and home footage into one 30–60 second video. You must:

Use the Razor Tool (or Ctrl/Cmd+K) at least once to split a clip
Trim at least two clips to adjust their length
Reorder clips — don't just put them in the order you filmed them
Create at least one moment where a cut from Space A (uni) to Space B (home) feels intentional, not random

No audio, music, or colour grading needed at this stage. Focus entirely on visual rhythm and edit logic.

1.5 hr
04

Write a Short Reflection

3–5 sentences. Describe: Which cut between the two spaces works best in your edit, and why? What connects the two spaces visually?

15 min
05

Instagram Post

Upload at least one clip to @layered_frames. In the caption, write one sentence describing the spatial relationship you were exploring.

10 min
Total estimated time: approx. 3.5 hours

Connecting to Your In-Class Prompt

Here are some ideas for how your home footage could relate to your in-class prompt — but feel free to find your own approach.

The Missing Person
The "missing" person appears in a domestic space — or their absence is felt through empty rooms, untouched objects.
A Repeating Day
The loop continues at home: the same morning routine, the same hallway, but something subtly shifts each time.
Behind the Scenes
Extend the "hidden spaces" idea to your own home — under beds, inside closets, behind appliances. What does a space look like when no one is watching?
Unexpected Encounter
After the encounter at uni, one character goes home. The domestic space reflects their emotional state — tidy vs. messy, bright vs. dark.
The Last Classroom
The search continues at home — opening doors, checking rooms, looking for something that may not exist.
