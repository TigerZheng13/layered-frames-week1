Chapter 01

Director Thinking

When AI does the easy work for you, you lose control over the things that actually matter — staging, action, and rhythm. The fix is to take that control back, prompt by prompt. This is the foundation everything else in this guide builds on.

Technique 01

Mise-en-scène — Depth, Motivated Light, Parallax

"Mise-en-scène" is the cinema term for how the director arranges everything inside the frame. When AI does it for you, you get a "safe" composition — flat, centred, lifeless. To take it back, you need to control three things: the depth of the space, where the light comes from, and how the camera moves through it.

01
Z-axis depth — build a room, not a wallpaper

Most beginners only describe X and Y axes (what's on the left, what's on the right, how tall the character is). This produces a flat sticker. Force three layers in your prompt: foreground (something obstructing), midground (where the action happens), background (environmental info). Three layers makes the frame a room, not a poster.

02
Motivated lighting — every light has a source

Don't write "dim lighting" or "moody lighting." Write where the light is coming from. Cool moonlight through left-side venetian blinds? A warm desk lamp lighting only half the character's face? Indoor light has politics — it picks sides, it carves the room, it tells the audience how to feel. Outdoor diffuse light written for an indoor scene is what creates AI's flat, characterless interiors.

03
Parallax movement — the camera passes through space

Most students prompt camera moves as "pan right" or "zoom in" — but to AI, those are just stretching a 2D image. They produce that plastic "fake camera" feel. Once you have layered depth, prompt truck right (the camera physically moves sideways behind the foreground obstruction). When the camera moves, the foreground blinds and the background bookshelf shift at different speeds — that parallax is the core of cinematic feel.

Mise-en-scène prompt formula
[Camera position + focal length] + [foreground obstruction / leading lines] + [precise midground subject + position] + [motivated indoor light source] + [background depth and environment]
AI-Driven Frame ✗
A handsome man, deeply sad, sitting in a dim cluttered room. Cinematic lighting, perfect composition, vintage furniture, oppressive atmosphere. Cinematic colour grade, 8k, masterpiece.
→ Character sits like a wooden post. Background is generic. Flat lighting. Looks like a moody wallpaper, not a film.
Director-Driven Frame ✓
Over-the-shoulder shot, 75mm medium telephoto. Foreground: blurred cool-toned venetian blind shadow stripes. Midground: a gaunt man sitting at a vintage desk, lit by a warm desk lamp (motivated light: only the left half of his face and the cluttered papers on the desk are illuminated). Background: blurred dim bookshelves and weathered walls. Mood: oppressive, isolated, restrained sadness.
→ Three-layer depth. Light has a source and a politics. Drama comes from how the room is staged, not from adjectives.
Video prompt — parallax camera move
Maintain the spatial depth and indoor lighting layout. The camera trucks slowly to the right, behind the foreground venetian blind shadows. The man's gaze stays fixed on the letter on the desk. The background bookshelves shift at a different speed than the foreground — natural parallax.
Pan
Camera stays still, only rotates. Like turning your neck — your eyes move, your body doesn't. No parallax.
Truck
Camera physically slides sideways through space. Like sitting in a moving car. Foreground and background move at different speeds — parallax.
Zoom
Camera stays still, lens changes focal length. Like using binoculars — you don't get closer, the image just enlarges. No parallax. Looks like a TV news effect.
Dolly
Camera physically moves forward or backward. Like walking toward the subject. Foreground grows faster than background — parallax.

Recommended camera movement vocabulary

Use the physical-movement words. AI's video models recognise them and will produce real parallax. Avoid the rotation/lens words unless you specifically want a flat or news-style look.

The simple rule

If the word describes the camera body moving through space, use it (truck, dolly, crane, tracking). If it describes the lens or the camera rotating in place, avoid it (pan, tilt, zoom) — unless that's specifically the look you want for a flat or surveillance-style shot.

Technique 02

Process Over Result — Direct Muscles, Not Emotions

The single most common mistake students make is writing the result of an emotion instead of the physical process that produces it. AI doesn't know what "sad" looks like in any specific way — it just stamps a generic sad face. But AI knows exactly what "eyes losing focus, lips trembling involuntarily, chest rising sharply with suppressed breath" looks like, because those are physical descriptions it can render.

Emotion as Result ✗
A young woman sits on a hospital corridor bench. After hearing terrible news, she feels extremely sad and despairing, tears streaming down her face. Photorealistic, cinematic light, 8k.
→ AI slaps two tears on a wooden expression. The performance is surface-level, with zero emotional impact.
Emotion as Process ✓
A young woman sits on a hospital corridor bench, close-up. Her eyes lose focus instantly. Her lips tremble involuntarily, then bite down hard on her lower lip. Her chest rises and falls violently from suppressed breathing. Her eye-rims redden rapidly and fill with tears. Photorealistic, cinematic light, 8k.
→ AI controls the four muscle groups (eyes, lips, breath, eye-rims) and delivers a fragile, breaking performance.

The companion principle: downgrade adjectives, upgrade verbs

When prompting action, replace adjectives ("lonely", "epic", "beautiful") with high-intensity verbs that fight against physical resistance. The character is no longer a backdrop — they're struggling against the world, and that's where story emerges.

Adjective Stacking ✗
An extremely lonely swordsman stands in a blizzard, the image is beautiful and desolate, epic cinematic feel, perfect composition, snowflakes drifting through the air, 8k.
→ AI generates a beautifully framed wooden post. No interaction with the storm. A pretty wallpaper, no story.
Verbs + Resistance ✓
A swordsman struggles forward against a blizzard. With frozen hands he firmly presses down his bamboo hat being lifted by the gale, his body tilted heavily forward, leaving deep footprints in the thick snow with each step. The wind and snow lash relentlessly against his clothing. Epic cinematic feel, telephoto compression, cool tones.
→ "Lonely" becomes "presses down hat against gale, tilts forward, leaves deep footprints." The character fights the world. Story emerges from action.
Action prompt formula
[Subject] + [high-intensity action verb / core action] + [physical resistance the action faces] + [micro-expression broken into muscle states — never emotion words]
The four facial control points

For close-up emotional shots, name four muscle groups: eyes (focus, redness, tears welling), lips (trembling, biting, pressing), jaw / chest (clenching, breathing), brow (furrowing, lifting). Direct each one. AI will deliver a performance.

Technique 03

Edit Over One-Shot — Don't Trust the Perfect 15 Seconds

Modern video models can generate a flawless 15-second long take from a single prompt. It will look smooth, continuous, technically perfect — and emotionally flat. That's because AI runs at a single uniform pace. Real cinema doesn't. Real cinema speeds up and slows down at emotional turning points, and that change in rhythm is what makes the audience feel something. The fix is to stop asking AI to make your film and start asking AI to make raw material for you to edit.

A concrete example

Imagine the scene: "A man walks into a café, orders a coffee, sits down, then suddenly sees an alien spaceship outside the window and stands up in shock."

One Prompt, 15 Seconds ✗
A man walks into a café, orders a coffee, sits down, sees an alien spaceship outside the window, then stands up in shock.
→ The motion is correct, but the moment of seeing the spaceship lands the same way as ordering coffee. No tension, no impact. Looks like a CCTV recording of an unusual event, not a film.
A-roll + Insert + B-roll ✓
Three separate generations, edited together: 1. Master shot (15s): the full action 2. Pupil ECU (2s): terrified eyes, dilated pupil 3. Cutaway (3s): phone falling, hitting floor
→ The cut to the pupil interrupts the calm. The falling phone gives the audience a beat to absorb the shock. You've engineered the rhythm.

The four-step workflow

01
Generate the master shot (A-roll)

Let AI do what it's good at — produce the smooth 15-second base sequence with the full action: walks in → sits down → sees spaceship → stands up. This is your raw material, not your finished shot.

02
Find the emotional break point — and cut

In your editor, scrub through the A-roll until you find the exact frame where the man first sees the spaceship. Cut the video there. Now you have two halves: the calm "before" and the reaction "after."

03
Generate the insert — the "stab"

Separately generate a high-impact extreme close-up: terrified eyes, dilated pupils, frozen expression. Use Nano Banana Pro to make the still, then convert it to a 2-second video. This shot doesn't exist in the master — you're making it just to interrupt the rhythm.

04
Generate the breathing space (B-roll)

Generate one more short clip that lets the shock land. A high-speed shot of a phone falling from a hand and hitting the floor. 3 seconds. This is your "breath" — the moment that gives the audience time to feel.

The final edit order

Timeline assembly
A-roll (master, first half) — man walks in, sits, looks up │ ▼ [HARD CUT at emotional break] Insert (pupil ECU, 2s) — terrified eyes, dilated pupil │ ▼ [HARD CUT] B-roll (phone falling, 3s) — phone slips, hits floor │ ▼ [HARD CUT] A-roll (master, second half) — man stands up in shock
The three-shot rhythm pattern

Master shot (A-roll) establishes — Extreme close-up (insert) stabs — Empty cutaway (B-roll) breathes. This pattern works for any emotional turning point: a phone call that changes everything, a confrontation, a discovery, a betrayal. Generate the three pieces separately. Edit them together. AI gives you the pieces — you control the rhythm.

Chapter 02

Why Your AI Footage Looks Plastic

The problem is never that AI isn't realistic enough. The problem is that it's too clean. Real cinema is messy — and that mess is exactly what's missing from your prompts.

Technique 04

Physical Foreground Occlusion

AI defaults to placing your subject dead-centre with nothing in front of them. That's why your shots look like wallpaper, not film. Real cinema uses foreground occlusion — leaves, pillars, doorframes, shoulders — to tell the audience where the camera is hiding. Without it, the camera reads as a "floating all-knowing eye" instead of a physical object in space.

Photo Thinking ✗
A beautiful girl, wearing a white dress, standing in a forest, highly detailed face, 8k, cinematic lighting.
→ Looks like a video-game render or a stock-photo wallpaper. Subject dead-centre, nothing between camera and subject.
Director Thinking ✓
Anamorphic lens, low angle. Extreme foreground: massive primal dark green leaves, blurred to the point of being abstract translucent shapes, partially blocking the frame, establishing a hidden voyeuristic POV. Through the gap, a lone woman lost in thought stands in an ancient mist-shrouded forest. Volumetric golden hour light. Strong shallow depth of field. Kodak Portra 400 film grain. Cinematic teal and orange.
→ Camera now has a position in physical space. The viewer feels they're peering through something, not just looking at it.
Foreground occlusion formula
[Camera spec: lens + sensor] + [extreme foreground blur as narrative medium] + [midground subject + action] + [background environment] + [film stock + colour grading]
Don't fake the environment

Don't add "leaves" to an indoor scene or "rain on glass" to a desert. AI will draw it, but the logic breaks and the audience reads it as fake. The foreground must belong to the world.

Technique 05

Atmospheric Occlusion — Make the Air Visible

AI footage often has a "vacuum feel" — characters look like they're in a sealed glass dome with the air sucked out. In reality, light always travels through something: dust, mist, smoke, particles. When you see a Makoto Shinkai frame, every shot has glowing motes drifting in light shafts. That's not a special effect. That's how real space looks.

Vacuum ✗
A young man standing by the window of a small office at sunset, Makoto Shinkai 2D anime style, highly detailed, 8k, clean and clear sky.
→ Razor sharp, vivid, and emotionally hollow. Character looks pasted onto the background. No air, no breath, no time passing.
With Atmosphere ✓
A young man standing by the large window of a small cluttered startup office at golden hour, looking out thoughtfully. Deep shadows, rich warm amber and soft purple grading, cinematic depth. Makoto Shinkai 2D anime movie style. Thick atmospheric haze filling the room. Tyndall effect (god rays) piercing through the windowpane. Microscopic dust motes floating and glowing warmly in the shafts of sunlight in the foreground.
→ Light has a path. Air has weight. The room breathes. The image suddenly carries time and emotion.
atmospheric haze氛围薄雾 volumetric light体积光 Tyndall effect丁达尔光 god rays耶稣光 dust motes微尘 floating particles漂浮颗粒 soft mist柔雾 light shafts光柱
Light without medium = fake light

Writing "cinematic lighting" or "god rays" alone gives you flat, painted-on light. The atmospheric medium (haze, dust, particles) is what makes the light visible in space. Always pair them.

Technique 06

Drop the "8K Detail" Obsession

The single biggest cause of plastic-looking AI footage is the reflex to stack words like 8k, ultra detail, sharp focus, clean, hyperrealistic. Every one of those words tells the AI to strip out atmospheric "imperfection" — exactly the imperfection that makes real images feel real. The result is sterile video-game CG.

Sharpness Stacking ✗
8k, ultra detailed, sharp focus, hyperrealistic, perfect lighting, clean composition, masterpiece
→ AI strips out grain, haze, softness, imperfections. You get a sterile, plastic, "render" look.
Embrace Imperfection ✓
Shot on Kodak Portra 400 film, natural film grain, slight motion blur, soft focus falloff, anamorphic lens, lived-in environment, candid feel
→ AI keeps the texture, breath, and softness that makes images feel photographed instead of generated.
Restraint on sharpness is what gives you cinema

Real film is never razor-sharp everywhere. There's grain, there's softness, there's depth-of-field falloff. Prompt for those, not against them.

Chapter 03

Controlling Light Like a Cinematographer

AI doesn't think in physics. It thinks in pixel patterns. That's why "light from the left" rarely works — but precise lighting terminology almost always does.

Technique 07

From "Source" to "Effect"

Most students write lighting prompts as a direction — "light from the left." But AI's left/right detection is unreliable because it doesn't actually understand 3D space. What it does understand is named lighting setups (Rembrandt, split, rim) and physical effects (long shadows, high contrast, hard rim). Use these instead.

Beginner
light from the left
Intermediate
side lighting, dramatic shadows on the right side of the face
Advanced
Rembrandt lighting, chiaroscuro, hard key light, long shadows, dark background, rim light catching the shoulder

Pair the term with its effect

Don't just say "Rembrandt lighting." Say "Rembrandt lighting, with a triangle of light on the cheekbone and the rest in shadow." Naming the effect anchors AI to the physics it can actually see in its training data.

Technique 08

De-light First, Then Re-light

When you want to change the lighting of an existing image, painting new light directly over the old light almost never works. The original lighting bleeds through. The professional move is to first ask the AI to de-light the image — flatten it back to even, neutral diffuse light — and then build new lighting on that clean baseline.

01
De-light the original

Prompt: "Remove all directional lighting from this image. Return the scene to flat, neutral, evenly-scattered ambient light. Preserve all subjects, props, and composition."

02
Mark the new light direction

Open the de-lit image in any drawing tool. Use coloured arrows to indicate where you want each light source to come from. Different colours for different lights.

03
Re-light with reference

Feed the annotated image back into the AI with a precise prompt referencing your colour-coded arrows.

Re-lighting prompt example
Apply Rembrandt lighting from the direction of the blue arrow. Add a soft fill light from the direction of the red arrow. Maintain the existing composition and subjects. Remove all arrows from the final output.
Why this works

Diffusion models follow pixel inertia. If the original image has strong shadows on the left, AI will keep painting shadows there even when your prompt says otherwise. Stripping the lighting first gives you a clean canvas.

Chapter 04

Telling AI Where Things Should Be

"From left to right" rarely works. AI's text-to-position signal is genuinely weak. But its image-to-position signal is overwhelming. Stop describing positions — start showing them.

Technique 09

The Visual Anchor Method

Diffusion models pay attention to feature matching (face shape, fabric texture) far more than spatial coordinates. Telling AI "the woman stands on the left and the man on the right" is a weak signal. Showing AI an image with two coloured boxes is a strong one. Three steps:

01
Generate an empty plate

Prompt only the location — no characters, no action. Just the space. This is your scene base.

02
Annotate by hand

Open the plate in any drawing tool — even your phone's screenshot editor. Draw simple boxes or arrows where you want characters to stand. Crude is fine.

03
Generate with reference

Feed the annotated plate AND your character image to AI. Prompt: place the character at the marked position. Always end with: "remove all boxes/arrows from the final image."

Single-character placement prompt
Place the character from image 1 at the position marked by the red box in image 2. Match the lighting and atmosphere of image 2. Blend the character naturally into the scene. Remove the red box from the final output.
Technique 10

Multi-Character Colour Isolation

With multiple characters, identical-coloured boxes confuse AI — it can't tell which character goes where. The fix is simple: use different colours for each character's marker, then explicitly map each colour to a character in your prompt.

Multi-character placement prompt
Place the character from image 2 sitting on the sofa marked with the red box in image 1. Place the character from image 3 sitting on the chair marked with the green box in image 1. Place the character from image 4 standing naturally at the position marked with the blue box in image 1. Relight all characters to match the scene's lighting. Remove all boxes from the final output.
Stick to high-contrast colours

Red, green, blue, yellow are safe. Avoid similar colours (orange and red, blue and purple) — AI will blend them. Three to four characters max in one frame using this method.

Technique 11

Motion Path Control for Video

The same logic applies to video generation. Want a character to walk a specific path? Don't describe it — draw the path on the empty plate as an arrow, feed the annotated image to your video model, and the character will follow the line.

Motion path video prompt
The character walks along the path of the red arrow in the image. Smooth natural movement, consistent pace. No arrows or lines should appear in the generated video.
Always specify "no arrows in the final video"

This is the single most common mistake. If you forget this line, AI will happily render the red arrow into your final video as a glowing red line on the floor. Add it to a negative prompt or repeat it for safety.

Technique 12

Multiple Characters, Different Paths

For complex video scenes with multiple characters moving differently — one walks left, one walks right, one stays still — combine colour isolation with numbered waypoints (point 1 → point 2). Be brutally explicit in the prompt.

Multi-character motion prompt
Three characters in the scene: red marker, green marker, blue marker. The green-marked character walks along the arrow from point 1 to point 2. The red-marked character walks along the arrow from point 1 to point 2. The blue-marked character stays in place, looking around slowly. No arrows or markers should appear in the final video.
This works in every tool

Visual anchor logic isn't tool-specific. It works in Nano Banana Pro, Midjourney, Kling, Hailuo, Veo, Runway. The annotation method is universal because all these tools share the same underlying weakness with text-based positioning.

The pattern behind everything

Every technique in this guide is a way of taking back control from the algorithm. AI defaults to flat, clean, centred, uniform. You override that by thinking like a director — staging the space, directing the muscles, editing the rhythm — then by using the specific techniques that fight AI's defaults: foreground obstruction, atmospheric haze, named lighting setups, and visual anchors instead of text descriptions of position.
