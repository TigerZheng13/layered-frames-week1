Lighting

As a designer, it's essential to craft compelling images by harnessing powerful lighting and skillfully manipulating it to enhance mood and visual impact.

Painters shape paint. Musicians shape sound. And cinematographers shape light.

Light is our medium. Before you press record, think about what that light will hit, and importantly, what it won't.
Natural light

Control, don't just capture

There are four ways to control natural light. You don't need any expensive gear to start practicing.

Diffusion

Apply diffusing paper or fabric over a window or light source to soften hard light and reduce harsh shadows.

Reflection / bounce

Use mirrors, reflectors, or white boards to redirect and bounce light into shadow areas or toward your subject.

Flagging / negative fill

Use black fabric or boards to block, cut, or absorb light. Sometimes taking away light is more important than adding it.

Shaping

Combine diffusion, reflection, and flagging to sculpt light precisely. Control where light falls and where shadow lives.

Fabric lighting

Diffusion vs bounce

The same fabric can produce completely different light depending on its thickness and how you use it.

1. Poly silk — diffusion
Semi-transparent synthetic fabric; light passes through and softens evenly
2. Muslin (thin) — diffusion
Loosely woven cotton; light passes through with slightly warmer, softer quality
3. Muslin (thick) — bounce
Light and subject on the same side; light hits the fabric and reflects back toward the subject
Artificial lights

Why filmmakers pursue soft light

The same tools (diffusion, reflection, flagging, shaping) apply to artificial lights. The goal is almost always to make hard artificial light behave like soft, natural light.

Natural feel

Soft light more closely resembles how light behaves in natural environments, especially indoors.

Flattering appearance

Reduces harsh shadows and skin texture. More complementary to faces.

Wrapping quality

Soft light "wraps around" the subject, creating dimensionality without harsh contrast.

Control

More even illumination, reducing problematic hot spots.

Good lighting is invisible. Bad lighting is impossible to miss.

Book Light Technique

A technique that creates ultra-soft light through double diffusion. Named "Book Light" because the setup resembles an open book. Light bounces off a reflective surface first, then passes through diffusion material. Each step increases the effective source size, producing progressively softer light with smooth shadow transitions.

Professional setup

Hard light source (fresnel, LED panel), reflector (white/silver card), diffusion material, and stands to position everything.

Low-budget solutions

Foam core as reflector, shower curtain or bed sheet as diffusion, clothespins for mounting, desk lamp as source.

Cove Lighting (Roger Deakins)

A curved wall of muslin placed around part of the subject, with multiple lights aimed at it. The bounced light wraps naturally around the subject, looking as if it comes from a practical light source in the scene. Used extensively by Deakins in films like 1917, Skyfall, and No Country for Old Men.

Mirror as a lighting tool

Mirrors redirect natural light to precisely light a space. Cheap acrylic mirrors work well. A clear frosted spray paint can add diffusion to reduce harsh hot spots from the mirror's reflection.

Light direction

Common lighting setups

Where you place your light relative to the subject completely changes the mood and feel of the image.

Frontal / flat lighting

Behind or above camera, same axis
Almost no shadows, flattening effect. Used for happy commercials and even-lit scenes.

Butterfly / beauty lighting

In front, higher than eye level, 45° down
Butterfly shadow under nose. Flattering, hides blemishes. Mostly for female subjects.

Rembrandt lighting

~45° to front, slightly elevated
Triangle of light on the shadow side. Dramatic, adds contrast and depth. Often for serious scenes.

Loop lighting

Similar to Rembrandt, slightly more frontal
Nose shadow doesn't connect to cheekbone. Less dramatic, still adds dimension.

Side lighting

Directly to the side (90°)
Half lit, half shadow. Strong contrast, dramatic and moody.

Kicker / edge / rim light

15-45° behind the subject
Bright edges around outline. Separates subject from background. Often warm-toned to match practicals.

Beauty / butterfly lighting

Key light placed high in front, angled 45° down. Creates a butterfly-shaped shadow under the nose and subtle cheekbone shadows.
