Part 1 — The Film

Required Content

Your final film must contain both of the following. The exact balance is up to you — what matters is that the two work together to create one coherent spatial story.

Required 01
Live-Shot Footage (min. 30%)

Real footage you have shot yourself. You may use parts of your Week 6 footage or shoot new material.

Required 02
AI-Generated Content

A substantial portion of your film must be AI-generated video using Veo, Luma, Kling, Runway, or any other AI video tool. Your AI content should serve the spatial story, not be a technology showcase.

Filmmaking Elements

What Your Film Must Demonstrate

Cinematography

Landscape orientation. Considered framing, composition, and camera movement that reflect director thinking rather than tool thinking — depth, motivated lighting, and parallax movement where appropriate.

Editing

Purposeful pacing and rhythm. Resist letting AI-generated long takes dictate your edit — break them, insert close-ups, use empty cutaways for breathing space.

Sound Design

Multi-layered audio: ambient bed + specific SFX + music. Silent or music-only AI films will not meet the standard.

Colour Grading

Considered colour grading that supports the mood and atmosphere of your film. Tonal choices should feel intentional and serve the story you're telling.

Working Together

Try to make your live footage and AI content feel like they're part of the same film, not two unrelated montages stitched together. Some unevenness is fine — what matters is that there's a story holding everything together.

Part 2 — Documentation

Pitch Deck (PDF, 10–12 pages)

Your pitch deck supports your film and is assessed alongside it. It should communicate your project's vision, process, and reflection clearly and professionally.

Title & Logline
Your film in one sentence. The world, the character, the transformation.
World Concept
What space did you transform, and why? What rules govern your world?
Mood Board
Visual references and the colour palette you developed.
Story Beats
The narrative or conceptual arc of your film. Even abstract films need an internal logic.
Sample Frames
4–6 still frames from your final film that represent the visual identity.
AI Documentation
Which AI tools you used, sample prompts, and how AI served your story (not just which buttons you pressed).
Process & Exploration
Show your journey, not just the destination. 2–3 directions you explored (including ones you abandoned), early prompts vs. final prompts, and at least one pivot moment where you changed your mind — and why.
Creator's Note
150 words. Your role(s), key stylistic choices, what worked, what was hard.
Final Presentation

Week 12 Screening

Friday 29 May · in class

Each student will screen their film and give a short pitch.

  • ~8 minutes per student: 3–5 min film + 1–2 min pitch + 1–2 min Q&A
  • Upload before class begins. Both your film and pitch deck must be in the OneDrive folder before screening starts, so the session runs smoothly.
  • Pitch your creative choices, not your workflow. Don't say "I used Runway then..." Tell us what you decided, and why.
  • Be ready for one or two questions. They're a chance to discuss your film, not a test.
Quality Bar

What We're Looking For

A film that is technically clean but emotionally hollow will not score well. The bar is set by these qualities, in this order:

Genuine spatial transformation — not just an AI demo with some real footage attached.

Storytelling or conceptual coherence — even abstract films need internal logic.

A coherent film — your live footage and AI content should feel connected by your story, even if they don't look identical.

Director thinking, not tool thinking — staging, motivated light, deliberate rhythm.

Personal voice — this should feel like your film, not a class assignment.
